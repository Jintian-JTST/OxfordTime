\title{Measurement of the Rydberg Constant from the Hydrogen Balmer Spectrum} 
\author{JINTIAN WANG, Exeter College, Oxford} 

\date{\today}  
\maketitle 

\begin{abstract} 
The visible emission spectrum of atomic hydrogen was investigated to determine the Rydberg constant and verify the quantization of atomic energy levels. A Czerny-Turner grating monochromator was first empirically calibrated using the known vacuum wavelengths of a mercury discharge lamp, employing a quadratic fit to accurately account for mechanical non-idealities in the instrument's sine-bar drive. Using this calibration, the wavelengths of the four visible Balmer series lines ($H_\alpha$, $H_\beta$, $H_\gamma$, $H_\delta$) emitted by a hydrogen discharge tube were measured. The Rydberg constant was calculated from a weighted mean of these transitions, yielding $R = 1.09828(22) \times 10^7 \text{ m}^{-1}$. While this demonstrates excellent agreement with quantum mechanical predictions, the experimental value exhibits a slight systematic overestimation of $0.14\%$ compared to the theoretical finite-mass constant, $R_H$, placing it numerically closer to $R_\infty$. This minor discrepancy is attributed to systematic uncertainties, predominantly a uniform zero-offset shift and residual mechanical backlash in the micrometer during the exchange of the light sources.\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

As quantum theory developed, energy levels of atoms were found to be quantized, and the emission spectra of atoms were found to consist of discrete lines. By measuring the wavelengths of these lines, the energy levels of atoms could be determined, and we can verify the predictions of quantum theory. In this experiment, we will use a mercury lamp as a source of light to calibrate a Czerny-Turner monochromator, and then measure the emission spectrum of a hydrogen lamp, and determine the wavelengths each line corresponds to.

\section{Theory}

In the Czerny-Turner monochromator, concave mirrors are used to collimate the beam of light falling on the grating and to focus those beams diffracted from the grating. The grating is mounted on a sine-bar mechanism, which allows the angle of the grating to be varied by turning a micrometer. By adjusting the angle of the grating, different wavelengths of light can be diffracted into the exit aperture, allowing us to select a specific wavelength for measurement.

According to \cite{op13}, the wavelength $\lambda$ of the light diffracted by the grating is given by
\begin{equation}
\lambda = z\left( \frac{2d\cos\gamma}{pl} \right),
\end{equation}
where $z$ is the sine-bar micrometer reading, $d$ is the grating spacing, $\gamma$ is the Czerny--Turner angle, $p$ is the diffraction order, and $l$ is the sine-bar length.

For the grating used in \cite{op13}, the spacing is $d \approx 1/(1200~\mathrm{mm^{-1}})=833~\mathrm{nm}$, which is of the same order as visible wavelengths ($\lambda \sim 400$--$700~\mathrm{nm}$). This confirms that first-order diffraction ($p=1$) produces the observed visible lines. Take the data of the monochromator in \cite{op13}, we can calculate coefficient $2d\cos\gamma/pl=2.000 \times 10^{-5}$.

According to the Bohr model of the hydrogen atom, the wavelength of the light emitted when an electron transitions from a higher energy level $n_i$ to a lower energy level $n_f$ is given by
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{\lambda} = R \left( \frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2} \right),
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the Rydberg constant. By measuring the wavelengths of the spectral lines of hydrogen, we can determine the value of the Rydberg constant and compare it with the theoretical value.

In this experiment, we will measure the wavelengths of the four visible Balmer lines of the hydrogen spectrum, which correspond to transitions from $n_i = 3, 4, 5, 6$ to $n_f = 2$. By substituting these values into equation (2), we can calculate the Rydberg constant $R$ for each line and take a weighted mean to obtain a more accurate value.


\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Calibration}

The monochromator was calibrated using the known vacuum wavelengths of the mercury emission spectrum. 
Although the sine-bar mechanism provides an approximately linear relation between micrometer reading $z$ and wavelength $\lambda$, mechanical imperfections and backlash in the drive mean that an empirical calibration is required.

With the mercury lamp aligned as described in figure \ref{spectra}, each visible spectral line was brought into coincidence with the cross-hair in the exit aperture. For each line, the micrometer was always approached from the same direction to eliminate backlash errors in the sine-bar mechanism.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{output.png}
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{mer.png}
\caption{Corresponding mercury spectrum \cite{op13}}
\label{spectra}
\end{figure}


Three readings were taken for each spectral line and averaged. The data were recorded in table \ref{calibration}. The slit width was kept fixed throughout the calibration procedure and was not altered subsequently, in order to avoid systematic shifts in the apparent line centre.

The averaged micrometer readings were then paired with the corresponding known vacuum wavelengths to establish the calibration relation between $z$ and $\lambda$.

\subsection{Measurement of the Hydrogen Spectrum}

After calibration, the mercury lamp was removed and replaced with the hydrogen discharge tube. The condensing lens and slit settings were left unchanged to ensure consistency with the calibration procedure.

The hydrogen source was aligned so that a uniform patch of light fell centrally on the grating. The cross-hair position was not altered after calibration, in order to preserve the established wavelength reference.

The four visible lines were identified and brought successively into coincidence with the cross-hair. As in the calibration procedure, all micrometer readings were approached from the same direction to minimise backlash effects. 

For each spectral line, multiple readings were taken and averaged. The data were recorded in table \ref{hydrogen}.

\subsection{Calibration}

The relationship is given by $z = a\lambda + b$, where $a$ and $b$ are the regression coefficients. The fitted line is shown in figure \ref{ccL}. The residual plot is shown in figure \ref{residual}.

It is obvious that the linear model fits the calibration data well, but the residuals show a quadratic pattern. This suggests that we should use a quadratic model to fit the calibration data. We then performed a quadratic regression on the calibration data using Python. The relationship is given by $z = a\lambda^2 + b\lambda + c$, where $a$, $b$ and $c$ are the regression coefficients. The fitted line is shown in figure \ref{ccQ}. The residual plot is shown in figure \ref{residualQ}. The residuals are randomly distributed around zero, indicating that the quadratic model is appropriate for the calibration data.

From figure \ref{residualQ}, we cannot see any pattern in the residuals, which suggests that the quadratic model fits the calibration data correctly. The residuals are distributed around zero, and mostlywithin the range from  -0.01 to 0.01, which is also the smallest resolution of the monochromator. Therefore, we can conclude that the quadratic model is appropriate for the calibration data. $R^2 = 0.999$ also indicates the goodness of fit of the quadratic model.

From the model we can obtain the three coefficients: $a = 2.856(251) \times 10^{-8}$, $b = 4.900(27) \times 10^{-5}$ and $c = 1.017(704) \times 10^{-1}$. These coefficients can be used to convert the micrometer readings of the hydrogen spectrum to wavelengths.



\subsection{Hydrogen wavelengths}

Since the calibration fit was performed in the form 
\( z = a\lambda^2 + b\lambda + c,\)
the hydrogen wavelengths were obtained by analytically inverting this quadratic relation for $\lambda(z)$ and selecting the physically relevant branch of the solution. Using the quadratic model obtained from the calibration, we can convert the micrometer readings of the hydrogen spectrum to wavelengths. The results are shown in table \ref{rydberg}.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering

\begin{tabular}{c c c c c c}

& $R$ ($10^7$\,m$^{-1}$) 
& $\sigma_R$ ($10^3$\,m$^{-1}$) \\
\hline\hline
$H_\alpha$ & 3 & 33.49(1) & $6560(2)$ & 1.09756 & 3.35 \\
$H_\beta$  & 4 & 24.56(1) & $4852(2)$ & 1.09920 & 4.53 \\
$H_\gamma$ & 5 & 21.90(1) & $4338(2)$ & 1.09772 & 5.06 \\
$H_\delta$ & 6 & 20.64(1) & $4093(2)$ & 1.09944 & 5.37 \\
\hline\hline
\end{tabular}
\label{rydberg}
\end{table}

The wavelength uncertainty was taken as the quadrature sum of the reading uncertainty in $z$ propagated through the calibration curve, $\sigma_{\lambda,z}=|d\lambda/dz|\sigma_z$, and the scatter of the mercury calibration about the fitted curve, $\sigma_{\lambda,\mathrm{cal}}$. In practice this is comparable to the instrument resolution, giving $\sigma_\lambda \approx 2$ \AA. Since we obtained $\sigma_R$ for each spectral line, a weighted mean of $R$ was calculated. 
The resulting value is

\[
\boxed{R = 1.09828 (22) \times 10^{7}\ \mathrm{m}^{-1}}.
\]

The uncertainty has been rounded to two significant figures, and the central value has been expressed to the corresponding precision.


\subsection{Theoretical prediction}

From values recommended in \cite{nist_constants},
\[
R_\infty = 1.0973732\times 10^{7}\ \si{m^{-1}},
\qquad
R_H = 1.0967758\times 10^{7}\ \si{m^{-1}}.
\]
Our measured $R$ is higher than $R_H$ by approximately $0.14\%$ and higher than $R_\infty$ by approximately $0.082\%$, suggesting a net systematic bias.



\subsection{Result comparisons}

The experimental value of the Rydberg constant is in excellent overall agreement with theoretical predictions. However, our result is slightly higher ($\approx 0.13\%$) than the theoretical $R_{\mathrm{H}}$. This small discrepancy is predominantly driven by systematic errors in the experiment, rather than statistical variance.

To illustrate this, consider a constant zero-offset error of $\Delta z = +0.02 \text{ mm}$ in the micrometer reading due to source replacement. Re-evaluating the wavelengths and resulting $R$ values under this uniform shift yields the adjusted values shown in Table \ref{rydberg_corrected}.

& $R$ ($10^7$\,m$^{-1}$) 
& $\sigma_R$ ($10^3$\,m$^{-1}$) \\

This conservative systematic shift yields a new weighted mean of $R \approx \boxed{1.09780 \times 10^7 \text{ m}^{-1}}$, demonstrating how sensitive the calculated constant is to minor mechanical offsets in the scale.


\section{Conclusion}

In this experiment, the discrete emission lines of the hydrogen Balmer series were successfully measured using an empirically calibrated Czerny-Turner monochromator. By applying a quadratic calibration curve to account for non-idealities in the sine-bar mechanism, the measurements yielded a weighted mean Rydberg constant of $R = 1.09828(22) \times 10^7 \text{ m}^{-1}$. While this result is in excellent overall agreement with quantum mechanical predictions, it exhibits a slight systematic overestimation of approximately $0.14\%$ compared to the theoretical finite-mass Rydberg constant $R_H$. This deviation renders the experimental value numerically closer to the infinite-mass constant, $R_\infty$, clearly indicating that the uncertainty budget is dominated by systematic deviations rather than purely random statistical fluctuations.

These systematic discrepancies are highly likely driven by a micrometer zero-offset, residual mechanical backlash, or a microscopic change in the illumination angle when the mercury calibration lamp was replaced by the hydrogen tube. As demonstrated in the analysis, a uniform $+0.02\text{ mm}$ shift in the micrometer readings corrects the experimental value to $R = 1.09780 \times 10^7 \text{ m}^{-1}$, moves the result towards $R_H$, but does not fully remove the offset. To improve the accuracy of future measurements, a rigorous zero-order alignment could be performed to physically quantify and eliminate the mechanical zero-offset. Furthermore, ensuring both light sources share an identical spatial footprint on the entrance slit, maintaining tighter control of the slit width, and increasing the number of calibration lines near the target wavelengths would help minimise wavelength-dependent aberrations and extrapolation errors.
